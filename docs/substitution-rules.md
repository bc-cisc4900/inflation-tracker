# Substitution Rules

## General Principle
The exact intended product should always be used when available. If the exact product cannot be found, the closest reasonable equivalent may be recorded.

## What Must Stay Similar
When making a substitution, the team should keep these as close as possible:
- product type
- package size
- item quality
- intended use
- unit price comparability

## Acceptable Example
If a specific brand of large organic eggs is unavailable, another brand of large organic eggs may be recorded if the package size and general product quality are comparable.

## Unacceptable Example
Do not substitute a clearly different product type just to fill the row. For example:
- do not replace white rice with flavored rice
- do not replace cheddar cheese with mozzarella
- do not replace organic apples with a very different premium prepared fruit product

## Trader Joe’s and Store-Brand Exceptions
Trader Joe’s often carries store-branded items only. In these cases, the team may record the closest store-brand equivalent if:
- the item type matches
- the size is close
- the product is still clearly comparable to the intended item

## Unit Price Focus
When exact branding is not possible, the team should still aim for fair price comparison by focusing on comparable size and type so the resulting unit price remains meaningful.

## Documentation Requirement
Every substitution should be documented in the notes field or notes column. The record should explain what was intended and what was actually used.

## Goal
The purpose of substitution rules is to preserve consistency without blocking data collection. A documented close match is better than forcing a bad comparison or leaving the dataset unclear.
